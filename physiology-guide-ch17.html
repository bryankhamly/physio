1The Big Picture: What Happens After You Inhale?

Chapter 16 covered how air gets into your lungs. But getting air into the lungs is only half the battle. Now we need to understand:

  1. How does oxygen get from the air in your alveoli into your blood?
  2. How does your blood carry oxygen to tissues?
  3. How does CO‚ÇÇ get from tissues back to the lungs?
  4. How does your body know when to breathe faster or slower?
üí° The Big Idea

Gas exchange works by diffusion‚Äîgases naturally move from areas of HIGH concentration (pressure) to LOW concentration. Your body doesn't actively "pump" oxygen; it simply creates the right conditions for diffusion to happen automatically.

üí≠ Think of it like perfume in a room...

If you spray perfume in one corner of a room, the scent molecules will naturally spread throughout the entire room‚Äîfrom high concentration to low concentration. This is diffusion. The same principle moves oxygen from air (high O‚ÇÇ) into blood (lower O‚ÇÇ), and moves CO‚ÇÇ from blood (high CO‚ÇÇ) into alveoli (lower CO‚ÇÇ).

2Understanding Partial Pressures: The Language of Gas Exchange

Before we can understand gas exchange, we need to understand a crucial concept: partial pressure. This is the key to predicting which way gases will move.

Dalton's Law: Air is a Mixture

The air you breathe isn't pure oxygen‚Äîit's a mixture of gases. Each gas contributes part of the total pressure:

Ptotal = PO‚ÇÇ + PCO‚ÇÇ + PN‚ÇÇ + PH‚ÇÇO + ...

Each gas's partial pressure depends on what percentage of the mixture it makes up

At sea level, atmospheric pressure is 760 mmHg. Since oxygen makes up about 21% of air:

Why Partial Pressure Matters

Each gas diffuses according to its OWN partial pressure gradient, independently of other gases. This is why oxygen and CO‚ÇÇ can move in opposite directions simultaneously!

üîë The Golden Rule of Gas Exchange

Gases diffuse from HIGH partial pressure ‚Üí LOW partial pressure. Always. No exceptions. If you know the partial pressures, you can predict exactly which way any gas will move.

3Gas Exchange: External and Internal Respiration

üí° The Big Idea

Gas exchange happens at two locations: in the lungs (external respiration) where blood picks up O‚ÇÇ and drops off CO‚ÇÇ, and in the tissues (internal respiration) where blood drops off O‚ÇÇ and picks up CO‚ÇÇ.

External Respiration (In the Lungs)

Blood arriving at the pulmonary capillaries is "deoxygenated"‚Äîit's been to the tissues and given up its oxygen. Now it needs to reload.

What Happens in the Lungs

1
Oxygen diffuses INTO blood

PO‚ÇÇ gradient: Alveoli (100) ‚Üí Blood (40). Oxygen moves from high to low, crossing the thin respiratory membrane into plasma, then into RBCs to bind hemoglobin.

2
CO‚ÇÇ diffuses OUT of blood

PCO‚ÇÇ gradient: Blood (46) ‚Üí Alveoli (40). CO‚ÇÇ moves out of the blood into alveolar air to be exhaled.

3
Equilibrium is reached rapidly

Blood is in the pulmonary capillaries for less than 1 second, but the thin membrane and huge surface area allow full equilibration. Blood leaves with PO‚ÇÇ = 100 mmHg.

Internal Respiration (In the Tissues)

Now oxygenated blood travels to systemic capillaries throughout the body. Here the process reverses:

1
Oxygen diffuses OUT of blood

PO‚ÇÇ gradient: Blood (100) ‚Üí Cells (‚â§40). Oxygen leaves blood to enter cells where it's used for cellular respiration.

2
CO‚ÇÇ diffuses INTO blood

PCO‚ÇÇ gradient: Cells (‚â•46) ‚Üí Blood (40). CO‚ÇÇ produced by cellular metabolism enters the blood for transport back to the lungs.

üîë Key Concept: Active Tissues Get More Oxygen

In very active tissues (exercising muscle), cells consume oxygen rapidly, dropping their PO‚ÇÇ to 20 mmHg or lower. This creates a STEEPER gradient, causing more oxygen to diffuse out of the blood. The system automatically delivers more oxygen where it's needed most!

  3. Partial pressure gradient: Bigger difference = faster diffusion. High altitude reduces the gradient (lower atmospheric PO‚ÇÇ).

6Control of Breathing: Why You Can't Hold Your Breath Forever

You breathe about 12-15 times per minute, roughly 20,000 times per day‚Äîmostly without thinking about it. How does your body control this automatic process?

üí° The Big Idea

Breathing is controlled by respiratory centers in the brainstem that set the basic rhythm. This rhythm is modified by chemoreceptors that detect changes in CO‚ÇÇ, pH, and O‚ÇÇ levels. The primary stimulus for breathing is CO‚ÇÇ (via pH), not oxygen!

The breathing rhythm originates in the brainstem, not the conscious brain:

When Does Oxygen Matter?

Oxygen only becomes a significant breathing stimulus when PO‚ÇÇ drops below about 60 mmHg‚Äîa dangerous level! This happens at very high altitudes or in severe lung disease.

Eventually, the brainstem's automatic drive to breathe overwhelms your voluntary control, forcing you to inhale. The urge to breathe is primarily driven by rising CO‚ÇÇ, not falling O‚ÇÇ!

7Ventilation-Perfusion Matching: Fine-Tuning the System

For optimal gas exchange, airflow (ventilation) and blood flow (perfusion) need to be matched. What good is well-ventilated alveoli if no blood flows past them, or vice versa?

üí° The Big Idea

The lungs have local control mechanisms that automatically match ventilation to perfusion. If an area has poor airflow, blood vessels constrict to redirect blood elsewhere. If an area has poor blood flow, airways constrict to redirect air elsewhere.

üîë Local Control Mechanisms

  • Low PO‚ÇÇ in alveoli ‚Üí Pulmonary arterioles constrict (redirects blood to better-ventilated areas)
  • High PCO‚ÇÇ in alveoli ‚Üí Bronchioles dilate (increases airflow to match blood flow)
  • Low PCO‚ÇÇ in alveoli ‚Üí Bronchioles constrict (reduces wasted ventilation)

üè• Why This Matters

This local control is unique to the pulmonary circulation! In systemic circulation, low O‚ÇÇ causes vasodilation (to bring more blood). In pulmonary circulation, low O‚ÇÇ causes vasoconstriction (to redirect blood to better-oxygenated areas). This "hypoxic vasoconstriction" is a clever adaptation that optimizes oxygen pickup in the lungs.
