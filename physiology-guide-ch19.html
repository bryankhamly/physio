1Body Fluid Compartments

Your body is about 60% water (for an average adult male). This water isn't just sloshing around randomly‚Äîit's organized into distinct compartments, and the balance between them is critical for proper cell function.

üí° The Big Idea

2Regulation of Water Balance: ADH

The primary regulator of water balance is antidiuretic hormone (ADH), also called vasopressin. ADH is produced by the hypothalamus and released from the posterior pituitary.

üí° The Big Idea

ADH controls how much water the kidneys reabsorb. More ADH = more water reabsorption = concentrated urine. Less ADH = less water reabsorption = dilute urine. It's like adjusting a faucet on your kidney's water recycling system.

The Thirst Mechanism

ADH conserves water you already have, but to gain water you need to drink. Three signals trigger thirst:

  1. ‚Üë Plasma osmolarity ‚Üí Osmoreceptors in hypothalamus
  2. ‚Üì Blood volume ‚Üí Angiotensin II stimulates thirst center
  3. Dry mouth/throat ‚Üí Sensory signals to brain

üè• Why Thirst Gets Suppressed Before Full Rehydration

You stop feeling thirsty after drinking even before the water is absorbed and corrects osmolarity. This "anticipatory" response from mouth/stomach stretch receptors prevents overdrinking!

üè• Clinical Connection: Diabetes Insipidus

Not to be confused with diabetes mellitus! In diabetes insipidus, either ADH isn't produced (central DI) or kidneys don't respond to it (nephrogenic DI). Result: massive amounts of dilute urine (up to 20 L/day!) and extreme thirst. Without treatment, severe dehydration can occur rapidly.

3Sodium Balance: Aldosterone & RAAS

While ADH regulates water, aldosterone is the primary regulator of sodium. Since sodium determines ECF volume, aldosterone indirectly controls blood volume and pressure.
